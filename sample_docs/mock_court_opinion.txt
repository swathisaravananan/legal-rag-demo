SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 22-1489

DATASTREAM ANALYTICS, INC., PETITIONER v. FEDERAL TRADE COMMISSION

ON WRIT OF CERTIORARI TO THE UNITED STATES COURT OF APPEALS FOR THE NINTH CIRCUIT

[March 15, 2024]

SYLLABUS

Petitioner DataStream Analytics collected granular behavioral data from mobile application users without obtaining explicit informed consent, allegedly violating Section 5(a) of the FTC Act, 15 U.S.C. 45(a). The FTC issued a cease-and-desist order and imposed civil penalties of $820 million. The Ninth Circuit affirmed.

We granted certiorari on: (1) whether collection of inferred sensitive attributes from behavioral metadata constitutes an unfair practice under Section 5(a) absent concrete consumer harm; and (2) whether FTC remedial authority extends to disgorgement of profits.

Held: Judgment affirmed in part, reversed in part.

1. Collection of inferred sensitive attributes without meaningful consent constitutes an unfair practice when it causes substantial injury consumers cannot reasonably avoid. DataStream's inference engine derived health status with 91% accuracy, political affiliation with 84% accuracy, and sexual orientation with 78% accuracy from behavioral patterns. These inferences were sold to data brokers without users' knowledge. Pp. 8-21.

2. The FTC's civil penalty authority under Section 5(m)(1)(A) does not extend to disgorgement of profits. AMG Capital Management, LLC v. FTC, 593 U.S. 67 (2021), forecloses such a reading. The $508 million disgorgement component is vacated. Pp. 22-31.

OPINION

ROBERTS, C.J., delivered the opinion of the Court.

DataStream operates mobile applications with 340 million monthly active users. Through an embedded SDK, DataStream captured behavioral signals and processed them through a machine learning model to infer sensitive personal attributes.

Section 5(a) of the FTC Act declares unlawful unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce. Congress amended the Act in 1994 to define unfair practices as those causing substantial injury not reasonably avoidable and not outweighed by countervailing benefits. 15 U.S.C. 45(n).

DataStream's 47-page terms of service buried a single sentence acknowledging third-party data partnerships in subsection 14(b)(iii). The FTC's consumer survey found 94% of users were unaware their behavioral patterns were used to infer medical conditions. The concrete risk of insurance discrimination, employment discrimination, and domestic abuse is precisely the harm Section 5(a) addresses.

The FTC imposed a total civil penalty of $820 million: $312 million in per-violation penalties under Section 5(m)(1)(A) and $508 million as equitable disgorgement. In AMG Capital, this Court held Section 13(b) does not authorize equitable monetary relief. The same logic extends to Section 5(m)(1)(A). Congress's failure to include express disgorgement authority reflects a deliberate legislative choice.

The $508 million disgorgement component is vacated. The matter is remanded for entry of a revised penalty order.

SOTOMAYOR, J., dissenting in part.

DataStream will retain $508 million in profits generated by conduct this Court holds violates federal law. The message to data brokers and platform companies is clear: violate the law, keep the profits. I respectfully dissent as to Part II.
